My name is Melany McEntee and I'm an incoming USU Master's student (Fall 2026) within the Department of Wildland Resources. Prior to beginning my coursework in the fall, I am starting field work this summer to begin collecting data for my thesis research. Under the guidance of Dr. David Stoner and in collaboration with Utah Division of Wildlife Resources (DWR), we will be calculating where recreationists may be at increased risk for moose conflict in Park City so we can conduct targeted outreach to try to keep the locals and their furry friends safer.

Why study moose in Summit County?

Park City is a useful setting for studying moose-human interactions because it combines high-quality moose habitat in the Wasatch Mountains with dense residential development, major road networks, and intensive year-round recreation.

As Utah’s human population and outdoor recreation continue to grow, encounters with moose are becoming more common across northern Utah mountain communities. While most interactions are harmless, conflicts do occur- especially vehicular collisions, encounters with domestic dogs, and situations where people unknowingly approach moose at close range.

Park City also experiences a high volume of visitors who may not be familiar with moose behavior or how to safely share space with large wildlife. That makes it an important location for understanding how human activity patterns influence encounter risk, and for developing effective outreach and management strategies.

Overall, Park City provides a strong case study for examining how expanding human use of mountain landscapes and persistent moose populations interact in a shared environment.
